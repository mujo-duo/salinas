Monitoring Unharvested Produce from Space:
A Peek into the Salinas Valley during COVID-19

This project was done as part of the NASA SpaceApps challenge.

Find Out More

Problem Statement

We are living in an unprecedented era where acres of farms are letting the crops wilt when most people are unemployed and going hungry. Due to sudden lockdown measures, millions of pounds of unharvested food is rotting on farms. This is due to a shortage of labor, lack of transportation, and market supply-chain disruption. There is an enormous disconnect between food cultivation and equitable distribution, which is amplified due to COVID-19 challenges. Our solution identifies when, and where crop is going past their regular harvest period. Determining this anomaly can help identify which crops have been most affected by the pandemic and how funds can be distributed to farmers. Moreover, policies can be designed to help collect and distribute food to those in need.

Approach

We utilized satellite data from NASA’s Landsat-8 that provides continuous earth observation. We derived the Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) from the near-infrared, red, and blue bands. This shows the ‘greenness’ index, which is used as a proxy to measure harvested and unharvested farms. We also utilized data from the California Crop Map from California Natural Resources Agency to identify crop-wise EVI for Salinas Valley. The end result shows the mean EVI values for each month of the year for the last 5 years. We identified a trend among all the crops that there is an unusual spike in the EVI in 2020 compared to the previous years, indicating unharvested farms. We developed the entire data processing pipeline using Google Earth Engine.

Findings

Strawberry
California grows about 88 percent of the US-grown strawberries along the California coast. All California strawberries are mostly hand-picked and are one of the most labor-intensive crops to harvest. During peak season, plants are harvested every three days. We see a steep uptick in strawberry EVI values indicating low harvest.
Leafy Greens
More than 90 percent of U.S. leafy greens production is located in California and Arizona. The majority of production from April through October occurs in the Salinas Valley of California. There have been several reports of farmers leaving the greens on the ground unharvested. We see an uptick in EVI values for leafy greens compared to the previous years.
Cole Crops
Cole crops include broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower, kale and other vegetables in the mustard family. Over 90 percent of broccoli production in the U.S. comes from California. Some Monterey County farmers have plowed under cole crops due to poor COVID-19 shutdown. In the plot, we see an increased anomaly in EVI for cole crops in Salinas Valley indicating low harvest.
Truck Crops
Truck farming is a practice of growing crops on a large scale for shipment to distant regions. California is one of the major truck farming areas. Due to COVID-19 shutdown, truck industry is significantly affected and many drivers are furloughed or laid-off. This chart shows an anomalous increase in EVI values for truck crops in 2020, indicating low harvest.

Our results show how the current harvesting practice compares to previous years, indicating that, around this time, most strawberries have started to be harvested, but this year a lot will be left behind. Determining the anomaly and quantifying the farm food waste can help identify which crops have been most affected by the pandemic and how funds and other aid can be distributed to farmers. Moreover, policies can be designed to help collect and distribute food to those in need. Even though the analysis is shown for Salinas Valley, this approach can be generally applied to other parts of the world. An extension of this work could be used to identify farm-level harvest changes.
